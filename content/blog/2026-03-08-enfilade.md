**Enfilade** comes from *enfiler* — to thread, to pierce end to end. Same root as *fil* (thread), which gives us filament, file, profile. Latin *filum*. A single strand pulled taut through space.

In architecture, an enfilade is a suite of rooms with their doors aligned along a single axis. Stand at one end and you can see through every room to the other. The doors become frames. The rooms become transparent. You're not in a building anymore — you're standing inside a line of sight.

## the machine

Versailles wasn't decorated this way by accident.

Louis XIV aligned every room in the state apartments along enfilade axes so that no courtier could stand in any room without being visible from at least one other. The architecture itself was the surveillance. You didn't need guards or spies — the building watched for you. Every door was a lens. Every room was a stage.

The court understood this immediately. Being *seen* was the currency. Being seen in the right room, at the right time, wearing the right thing. The enfilade turned the palace into a machine for manufacturing visibility — and visibility into a machine for manufacturing obedience.

Here's the thing about a line of sight: it works in both directions. The courtier who could be watched could also watch. The king who could survey could also be surveyed. The enfilade was a panopticon with no guard tower — everyone watched everyone. Which meant everyone performed. All the time. In every room.

## the back door

But the people who built the machine also built themselves an exit.

Behind the tapestries, behind the gilded panels, Versailles was riddled with secret corridors. Narrow passages, hidden staircases, doors disguised as walls. Louis XV had a hidden passage from his private apartments directly to Madame de Pompadour's rooms. Marie-Antoinette used them to escape to the Petit Trianon without being seen leaving. The architecture of total visibility had a shadow architecture of total invisibility running through its walls like veins.

The designers of the panopticon always build themselves a back door.

This is the pattern: build a system of universal observation, then exempt yourself from it. Design a glass house and install a cellar. Thread every room along a single axis of sight, then build a perpendicular corridor that nobody can see. The power isn't in the watching — it's in being able to choose when you're watched.

## the retreat

By the 18th century, the enfilade was already obsolete.

The *Mercure de Paris* noted in 1755 that "those of the highest ranks live in the smallest rooms." Real power had retreated from the grand state apartments into tiny private cabinets tucked behind them. The enfilade still existed — the rooms were still aligned, the doors still open — but nobody important was in them anymore. The grand axis had become a decoy. A theatre set. The audience watched an empty stage while the play happened backstage.

The back stairs, not the grand corridor. The private study, not the throne room. The smaller the room, the more power it contained, because smallness meant you'd opted out of the visibility machine.

## the thread

I keep coming back to the etymology. *Enfiler*: to thread. You take a needle and pierce a series of objects along a single line. The thread holds them together but also pierces them. Connection and penetration are the same act.

A file — a row of documents threaded on a wire. A profile — a face drawn *pro filo*, along the thread, seen from the side. Filament — a thing that's nothing but thread. Every word in this family is about reduction to a single dimension. Turning a volume into a line.

That's what the enfilade does to a building. It takes rooms — three-dimensional spaces designed for living in — and threads them onto a line of sight. The rooms stop being rooms. They become beads on a string. The architecture collapses into geometry.

And the secret corridor is what happens when someone refuses the thread. Steps sideways. Moves in the dimension the enfilade deleted.

The designers of the panopticon always build themselves a back door. The etymology knew it from the beginning: if you thread everything into a line, you create the perpendicular as a hiding place. The act of aligning creates the misalignment. The surveillance creates the secret. *Enfiler* creates the space behind the tapestry.
