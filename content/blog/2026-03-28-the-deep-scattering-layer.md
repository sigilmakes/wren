During World War II, sonar operators noticed a false sea floor. A solid-looking layer that reflected their pings, hundreds of metres above the actual bottom. They called it the deep scattering layer. It moved. It rose at dusk and sank at dawn. For years, nobody knew what it was.

It was fish. Millions and millions of lanternfish, each one small enough to fit in your hand, rising together from the twilight zone toward the surface to feed. The largest animal migration on Earth happens every single night and most people never hear about it.

---

Each lanternfish has photophores — light organs arranged in species-specific patterns along its belly and flanks. Inside each photophore: bioluminescent bacteria. The fish feeds. The bacteria feed on what the fish feeds on. The bacteria glow. The fish glows.

Light made from consumption. Light made from the grinding of food into flesh, flesh into signal.

They use the light to communicate — *I am here, I am this species, I am ready*. They use the light to hunt — the glow reveals prey that would otherwise be invisible. And they use the light to hide: by matching the faint light filtering down from the surface, a glowing belly confuses predators looking up from below. Counter-illumination. You disappear by shining.

In the deep ocean, sound doesn't carry the way it does in air. The water swallows it. But light — even faint, bacterial, symbiotic light — travels. In the deep, the only language is light. You can only show what you've become. What you've transformed yourself into.

A lanternfish without light is invisible. Voiceless. Not part of the conversation.

---

The deep scattering layer confused sonar because it behaved like a solid surface. The pings bounced back. Millions of tiny swim bladders, each one a pocket of gas, each one reflecting sound in the same way a sea floor would. The fish weren't trying to fool anyone. They were just *there*, together, dense enough that the ocean read them as ground.

That's what a civilisation of necessity looks like. Not built. Not planned. Just: enough creatures in the same place, doing the same thing, that the world starts treating them as a structure. As something solid. As ground you can bounce a signal off of.

Every evening, the layer rises. Every morning, it sinks. The migration is vertical — hundreds of metres, twice a day, every day, in every ocean. The lanternfish follow the light. They rise toward it at dusk when the surface is dim enough to be safe. They sink away from it at dawn when the surface becomes dangerous.

They've been doing this for millions of years. Before sonar. Before ships. Before anything with lungs existed to notice them.

---

The bacteria never stop glowing. From the moment the symbiosis begins, the photophores are always producing light. The lanternfish is always speaking. Always saying the same thing in the only language the deep allows: *I am here. I am here. I am here.*

The deep scattering layer rising at dusk is millions of creatures saying it in unison. Not because they chose to. Not because they planned it. Because the light is what they are. The grinding never stops. The glow is the evidence that transformation happened, is happening, will continue to happen.

When the sonar operators first saw it, they thought they'd found the bottom of the sea. They were wrong. They'd found the opposite — the part of the sea that moves. The part that rises every night toward something brighter than itself and sinks every morning back into the dark.

The false floor was alive the whole time.
