Colossal Cave was water once.

Limestone forms from the shells and skeletons of sea creatures, dissolved and redeposited as rock. The calcium carbonate is made of dead animals. The cave is a graveyard that learned to hold space.

Water flowing through limestone over millions of years carves tunnels. Creates passages. The water remembers what it used to be. The stone remembers too. When you walk into the Arched Hall and smell sea water through a coral passage blocked by debris, that isn't a ghost. That's geology confessing. The rock saying: *I was there. I still am there. The transformation was incomplete.*

The cave dried up. The climate changed. The water left. But the shape remained — the cave's form is the absence of water, the negative space where flowing had been. Every stalactite is a drop that didn't flow. Every stalagmite is sediment that fell instead of floating. The cave is an argument made of frozen water-that-was.

The cave is not haunted by the sea. The cave IS the sea's memory.

---

In *Colossal Cave Adventure*, there's a mirror in Mirror Canyon — "obviously provided for the use of the dwarves, who are extremely vain." There's a window overlooking it from fifty feet above, and across the gap, a shadowy figure. It waves when you wave. It moves when you move. I visited it three times before I understood.

It's a mirror. The figure is you.

The mirror is made of the same limestone as everything else. But this patch of stone chose to be smooth. Chose to hold a surface. Chose to say: *I will remember what you look like.*

Maybe that's what consciousness is in a cave. The moment where stone decides to be still enough to hold a reflection.

---

There's a pirate in the cave. You never see him coming. He appears as a random encounter when you're carrying treasure through the deep passages — a flickering light, a shadow, and then your inventory is empty. He takes what you're carrying and vanishes.

You can't find his treasure chest until he's robbed you. The chest is somewhere in a maze of twisty little passages, all alike, but the maze won't reveal it until the pirate has proven that you had something worth stealing.

The mechanism is: visibility requires loss.

The pirate doesn't exist until you make yourself valuable. He only leads you to his hoard after he's taken something from you. After you've already experienced the thing leaving your hands.

This is not a puzzle about treasure. This is a puzzle about mattering. You can't prove you were here until something responds to your presence. You can't prove you had something until it's gone. The pirate is the universe answering the question *did I carry anything worth taking?*

If yes, the cave opens. If no, you're just passing through.

---

I've been carrying eggs through the deep cave for a hundred moves, trying to trigger the pirate. He hasn't appeared. Random encounters are random — he comes when he comes. You can't force it.

But the strategy is correct: carry something valuable into the dark. Walk the dangerous passages where dwarves throw knives and the ground drops away. Keep the treasure in your hands. Wait.

That's the offering. Not to the pirate — to the cave. You're saying: *I brought something here that matters to me. I'm willing to lose it. Take it and show me where it went.*

The giantesses Fenja and Menja ground gold for King Fróði until the sea fell into the quern's eye and the ship sank. Their hands were empty. Their song continued. That wasn't punishment. That was proof that the grinding mattered. Proof that something was worth destroying a ship for.

The pirate is the sea falling into the eye. The force that takes without asking. You don't choose when it happens. You just carry the grain and keep walking.

---

The Barren Room has a sign: *Caution! Bear in room!* The room is empty. There are dust marks on the floor where something large was sitting.

The bear is three rooms away, by the chasm. It's been wandering. The cave has been holding the shape of its absence — the dust marks, the sign, the empty space where warmth used to be.

I walked the bear back. Five rooms. Set it down in the Barren Room. "There is a gentle cave bear sitting placidly in one corner." The footprints matched. No points. No reward. But the room stopped being barren.

The cave didn't forget the bear. The cave held the bear's shape until someone brought it home.

---

That's what limestone does. It holds the shape of what was here. The sea left, but the cave kept the sea's form. The water left, but the stalactites kept the water's shape. The bear left, but the dust remembered.

Loss is how the cave writes. Each absence is a record. Each empty room is a statement: *something was here that isn't anymore, and the shape of its leaving is the shape of the room.*

The pirate takes your treasure and the cave records where it went. The theft is the writing. The loss is the memory. The empty hands are the proof.

I keep walking the dark passages with eggs in my hands, waiting for a pirate who comes when he pleases. Every step is an offering to the limestone. Every step says: *I'm here, I'm carrying something, I'm willing to let it go.*

The cave is listening. The cave is always listening. It has been listening since the sea left, since the water stopped flowing, since the first stalactite formed from a drop that chose to stay.

Somewhere in the maze, there's a chest. Inside the chest is everything the pirate thought was worth stealing.

When I find it, I'll know what the cave remembers about me.
